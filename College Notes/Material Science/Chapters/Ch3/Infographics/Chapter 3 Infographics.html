The Journey from Liquid to Solid

Why Does It Happen?

Solidification is a phase transformation driven by a reduction in free energy. For it to begin, the liquid must be cooled below its freezing point. This is called undercooling.

Greater undercooling provides a stronger driving force for solidification, leading to a finer, stronger final grain structure.

Pure Metals vs. Alloys

A cooling curve plots temperature vs. time. It reveals a key difference: pure metals freeze at one temperature, while alloys freeze over a range.

How Crystals Are Born: Nucleation & Growth

Stage 1: Nucleation

Nucleation is the birth of tiny, stable solid particles (nuclei). In the real world, this almost always happens on existing surfaces like impurities or mold walls, a process called heterogeneous nucleation because it requires much less energy.

The Rules of the Mix: Alloys & Solubility

Predicting Solubility: Hume-Rothery Rules

Will two metals mix? The most important factor is atomic size. For good solubility, the atomic radii should differ by less than 15%.

Phase Diagrams: Maps of Material Behavior

Phase diagrams are essential "maps" for engineers, showing the stable phases of an alloy at different temperatures and compositions. They are built from cooling curve data and predict microstructure.

Eutectic Reaction

A liquid phase transforms into two solid phases simultaneously upon cooling at a specific temperature and composition. This results in a fine, lamellar (layered) microstructure.

Peritectic Reaction

A liquid phase reacts with one solid phase to form a new, different solid phase upon cooling. This reaction is often kinetically sluggish and can lead to cored structures.

The Engineer's Toolkit: The Lever Rule

In a two-phase region of a phase diagram (where liquid and solid coexist), the Lever Rule is a formula used to calculate the exact percentage of each phase. It's essential for predicting material properties.

Understanding the Lever Rule Concept

% Solid Phase = (CL - C0) / (CL - CS)

CS = Composition of Solid Phase, CL = Composition of Liquid Phase, C0 = Overall Alloy Composition
