Power Screws

The Unseen Force Turning Rotation into Linear Power

1. The Anatomy of a Power Screw

Key Terminology

A power screw is essentially a ramp wrapped around a cylinder. Its geometry dictates its performance. Understanding these terms is the first step to mastering their design.

  • Pitch ($p$): The distance between adjacent threads.
  • Lead ($L$): The linear distance the screw travels in one full revolution. For multi-start threads, $L = (\text{No. of starts}) \times p$.
  • Helix Angle ($\lambda$): The angle of the thread's incline. A smaller angle means more force multiplication.

3. Deeper Dive: Advanced Considerations

Beyond the Basics

  • Material & Lubrication

    The choice of materials (e.g., steel screw with a bronze nut) and proper lubrication is crucial. Lubrication minimizes the coefficient of friction ($\mu$), which increases efficiency and reduces wear and heat generation.

  • Back-driving & Overhauling

    A power screw is "overhauling" if the load can drive the screw backward without any external torque. This occurs when the helix angle is greater than the friction angle ($\lambda > \phi$). This is a critical safety failure mode to prevent in applications like jacks.

Key Design Formulas

Torque to Raise a Load ($T_R$)

$$ T_R = \frac{W \cdot d_m}{2} \left( \frac{L + \pi \mu d_m}{\pi d_m - \mu L} \right) $$

Torque to Lower a Load ($T_L$)

$$ T_L = \frac{W \cdot d_m}{2} \left( \frac{\pi \mu d_m - L}{\pi d_m + \mu L} \right) $$

Efficiency ($\eta$)

$$ \eta = \frac{T_{ideal}}{T_{actual}} = \frac{W \cdot L}{2\pi T_R} $$

where $W$ is the load, $d_m$ is the mean diameter, $L$ is the lead, and $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction.

4. Will It Break? Understanding Stresses

Where Does It Hurt?

A power screw experiences multiple types of stress simultaneously. An engineer must analyze all of them to prevent failure.

Primary Stress Types

  • Tensile/Compressive Stress

    Caused by the axial load trying to pull apart or crush the screw's core.

  • Torsional Shear Stress

    Caused by the input torque trying to twist the screw shaft.

  • Bearing Stress

    The crushing pressure on the contact surfaces of the screw and nut threads.

  • Thread Shear Stress

    The stress that could cause the threads themselves to shear off from the screw body.
